Most IT pros can tell you what a firewall does. Fewer can tell you what a successful attack actually looks like once it's already inside the network — because that story is written in the logs, and almost nobody reads them until something's already on fire.

You don't need to be a SOC analyst to get real value out of Windows Event Logs. You need to recognize a small, high-signal set of Event IDs and know what question each one answers.

None of these are exciting in isolation. A 4624 fires constantly — every legitimate logon generates one. The signal isn't in any single event; it's in the pattern. A 4625 storm followed by a 4624 on the same account is a brute-force that landed. A 4688 spawning powershell.exe -enc from a process that has no business touching PowerShell is worth stopping to look at.

Why This Matters Without a SOC

You probably don't have a 24/7 detection team combing through this for you. That's fine — you don't need volume, you need recognition. If you can glance at a log and immediately know "that's a 4720 I didn't expect" or "that 1102 wasn't me clearing anything," you've already outpaced most of the org.

The goal of this lesson isn't to turn you into an analyst overnight. It's to make sure the next time you're staring at Event Viewer during an incident, you're not starting from zero.
